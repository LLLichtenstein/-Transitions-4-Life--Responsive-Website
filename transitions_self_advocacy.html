SELF-ADVOCACY

Definition of SELF-ADVOCACY : knowing what you want, knowing what you are entitled to, and how you can effectively reach your goals emphasizing personal rights and responsibilities, effective communication of needs, and negotiation for help


Self-Advocacy SKILLS :

  • knowing your skills, strengths, and weaknesses
  • asking for what you need
  • negotiating for yourself
  • knowing your rights and responsibilities
  • using the resources that are available to you
  • being able to explain your disability

LAWS


During High School the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) provides free instructional and support services through an Individualized Education Plan.

When transitioning to college or work, the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) applies.

COLLEGE


The most recent version of ADA, Americans with Disabilities Act Amendments Act (ADAAA), and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act apply. These civil rights laws mandate that colleges provide access to accommodations to all otherwise qualified students and afford them an equal opportunity in the institution's programs, activities, and services.

WORK


The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA) requires employers to provide reasonable accommodation to qualified employees and applicants with disabilities, unless such accommodations would pose an undue hardship. An undue hardship might be that the accommodation is too costly, too extensive, too substantial, or too disruptive.

THE PROCESS

COLLEGE


Availability, purchase, and/or use of assistive technologies in college that were used in high school requires students to qualify under the law.

In order to do so, students must self-identify or disclose as having a disability. Documentation is provided to the student disability services office at the college. The college may require an updated evaluation.

WORK


In general, the applicant or employee with a disability is responsible to advise the employer that an accommodation is needed in order to participate in the application process, to perform essential job functions, or to receive equal benefits and privileges of employment.

Employers are not required to provide accommodations if they are not aware of the need.

WHO PAYS

COLLEGE


The college may pay; or private insurance, a non-profit, or a grant.

WORK


The employer pays for reasonable accommodation; that is, the employer is not put in a financial hardship.

ASSISTIVE TECHNOLOGY

COLLEGE


Common Assistive Technologies:

  • alternative mouse device and keyboard
  • text-to-speech application
  • speech recognition software
  • smart pen
  • digital recorder
  • graphic organizer software
  • talking calculator
  • screen magnifier

WORK


Common Assistive Technologies:

  • voice recognition software
  • word prediction software
  • adapted computer setup
  • amplified phone or text telephone

ACCOMODATIONS

COLLEGE


Supports and services provided on college campuses so qualified students with disabilities have equal access and opportunity to benefit from classes, programs, and activities.

Common Accommodations:

  • extended time
  • oral testing
  • scribe (for writing)
  • low-distraction testing environment
  • large print
  • sign language interpreter
  • Braille material

WORK


Any modification or adjustment to a work environment that enables a qualified person with a disability to apply for or perform a job.

Common Work Site Modifications:

  • adjustable desks and seating
  • ramps
  • lifts
  • wall partitions around workstations to minimize distractions
